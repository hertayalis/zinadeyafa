'And Just Like That...' Responds to Fan Backlash Over Steve's Storyline

Publish date: 2024-08-11

Members of the And Just Like That have addressed the show's treatment of Steve Brady.

Since he first met Miranda in Sex and the City, Steve, played by David Eigenberg, has been a fan favorite and considered one of the best love interests of the original series. Now, on the series' revival, the lovable bar owner has been unrecognizable to fans in the few scenes he has, with the now absent-minded character mostly taken a backseat to his wife's affair with nonbinary comedian Che.

Last week's episode saw Miranda try to reignite her marriage after being ghosted by Che. In an awkward redux of the kitchen hookup, Steve seems to forget how to pleasure his wife, and a disappointed Miranda declares their sex life dead and goes back to Che. Viewers have taken to Twitter to argue that Steve deserves better, pointing out that he hasn't had a real chance to communicate with Miranda.

In a Vanity Fair interview, And Just Like That... executive producers Julie Rottenberg and Elisa Zuritsky, who both wrote for SATC, revealed that they knew Miranda's affair would be divisive, with the some of the show's crew even printing up Team Steve and Team Miranda buttons during production. (Eignenberg reportedly picked Team Miranda).

"We love Steve," Zuritsky explained, debunking theories that the writers had a grudge against the character. "We are really invested in the Steve-ness of him. He’s so full of life, and the Steves out there are good guys."

"But Miranda’s journey is representing another reality out there, which a lot of people go through—the reevaluations and transitions in life," added Rottenberg. "Grown couples grow apart, and people come to epiphanies about what their spouse is or isn’t fulfilling for them. Miranda’s story was very representative of a certain path that a lot of women find themselves on."

"We didn’t set out to make virtuous characters necessarily," Zuritsky said. Even beloved people have crises. Even moral, generally wonderful people make choices that aren’t necessarily admirable or virtuous. But they do them anyway because they’re going through something, or they’re working through a crisis."

She continued, "sometimes your friends make choices that you might not agree with or that might be concerning, but you sort of have to let them make their own choices. Sometimes it’s hard."

The writers also addressed Steve and Miranda's relationship going forward, and whether they'll have an actual conversation about Miranda's affair and the state of their marraige. Zuritsky assured, "You’re going to get that scene."
